The strongest historical ancestor is EP0826100B1, “Process for the selective lambda control of a cylinder in a multi-cylinder internal combustion engine.” It was filed by Siemens in 1996 from a 1995 priority date, granted in 1999, and Google Patents lists it as “Expired - Lifetime” with an anticipated expiration on 2016-05-02. The WO counterpart is also marked ceased. 

What makes it the closest ancestor to your split-bank LPG idea is its structure. The patent explicitly starts from the limitation of a conventional narrowband lambda probe: it sees the combined exhaust from multiple cylinders, responds slowly, and therefore cannot tell whether one cylinder group is rich while another is lean. To solve that, it proposes separate exhaust lines / banks with separate lambda sensors, and it describes the engine as having two cylinder banks with sensors LS1 and LS2, positioned close to the engine to improve cylinder-selective detection. It also says the number of exhaust lines and sensors can scale with the number of cylinder groups. 

The control idea is also very close to what you are doing conceptually. It uses two control loops: an outer loop for the global mean lambda, and an inner loop for cylinder-selective lambda control. The sensor signals are sampled with cylinder identity and a timing delay based on load and RPM, and the patent mentions signal linearization maps for converting sensor voltage to air ratio. In other words, it is not just “read lambda and correct fuel”; it is “read each bank’s lambda at the right crank-angle window and trim that subgroup separately,” which is the same conceptual family as a split-bank controller. 

Its “fate” is best understood in two layers. Legally, the patent simply ran its term and expired. Technologically, the underlying idea did not disappear: later patents generalized the same banked rich/lean concept into split lambda fueling operations, and commercial LPG systems still expose bank 1 / bank 2 lambda connections and bank-aware calibration parameters. That suggests the ancestor’s ideas were absorbed into mainstream engine-control and LPG practice rather than remaining a standalone, widely remembered product line.
